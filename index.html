Ill Effects of Protein Powders

Hold to reveal health risks
6 Documented Health Risks of Commercial Protein Supplements
🦴

Disorders of Bone & Calcium Homeostasis

Excessive protein intake increases urinary calcium excretion, leading to reduced bone mineral density. High-protein diets create an acid load that the body neutralises by leaching calcium from bones, increasing risk of osteoporosis over time.

📄 Sellmeyer et al., 2001 — AJCN (PubMed)
🫘

Disorders of Renal Function

Prolonged high protein intake increases glomerular pressure and hyperfiltration. In individuals with pre-existing kidney conditions, this can accelerate chronic kidney disease progression and increase serum creatinine and urea levels.

📄 Knight et al., 2003 — Annals of Internal Medicine (PMC)
🔬

Increased Cancer Risk

Many commercial protein powders have been found to contain heavy metals (lead, arsenic, cadmium) and BPA from plastic packaging. The Clean Label Project (2018) found detectable levels of contaminants in popular brands, raising long-term cancer concerns.

📄 Consumer Reports — Protein Powder Safety Review
🫃

Digestive Issues

Lactose (in whey concentrates), artificial sweeteners like sorbitol and sucralose, and thickeners like xanthan gum can cause bloating, gas, diarrhoea, and cramping. Whey protein has been implicated in exacerbating Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS).

📄 Rubach et al. — Dairy Proteins & GI Distress (PMC)
🫀

Disorders of Liver Function

The liver metabolises excess amino acids and is responsible for detoxifying additives found in supplements. Very high protein intake strains hepatic pathways. Several case studies document elevated liver enzymes and acute liver injury linked to supplement use.

📄 Timcheh-Hariri et al. — Protein Supplement Hepatotoxicity (PMC)
⚖️

Hormonal & Metabolic Disruption

Many protein powders contain anabolic additives, growth hormones, and phytoestrogens (in soy-based products). These can interfere with the endocrine system, affecting thyroid function, insulin sensitivity, and hormonal balance in young adults.

📄 Messina et al. — Soy Phytoestrogens Review (PMC)
